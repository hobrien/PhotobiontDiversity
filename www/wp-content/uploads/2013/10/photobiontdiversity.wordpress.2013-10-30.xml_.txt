  <content:encoded><![CDATA[As with most organisms, DNA sequencing has revolutionised our understanding of the genetic diversity and phylogenetic relationships of lichen photobionts over the last two decades. However, unlike most organisms, these insights have rarely been translated to formal taxonomic changes and thus, no comprehensive system exists to organize the diversity that has been uncovered. Studies focus on different taxonomic scales, sequence different markers and use different analyses. Even when the methods are consistent, comparisons are rarely made to all related sequences in the database. Studies that do attempt a comprehensive analysis, such as <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/store/10.1111/nph.12165/asset/supinfo/nph12165-sup-0001-FigS1-S2.pdf?v=1&amp;s=093bbe5bc160eb640def4b52dd02c77060de4965">this</a> one are hopelessly out of date by the time they are published.

The goal of this blog is to provide a real-time snapshot of the current state of knowledge of genetic diversity in lichen photobionts and related organisms. I am collecting DNA sequences of photobionts and related organisms a <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity">repository</a> , along with associated metadata and phylogenies. Annotated phylogenies and descriptions are posted whenever new data become available or when I think of something interesting to say about them.   <title>Obtaining the sequences</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/04/25/obtaining-the-sequences/</link>
  <pubDate>Thu, 25 Apr 2013 11:40:08 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=37</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Perhaps not surprisingly given my <a href="http://books.google.co.uk/books/about/Phylogenetics_of_Heterocystous_Cyanobact.html?id=ZdLlNwAACAAJ&amp;redir_esc=y">background</a>, I will be starting with <em>Nostoc</em> photobionts. In my opinion, the most useful marker for this group is <em>rbcX</em>, so I will be starting there.

<span style="line-height:1.5;">I have decided to use blast to obtain all sequences that are homologous to a reference sequence. This will allow me to catch sequences that have been mis-labeled and/or sequences from organisms that have been mis-identified. What better reference sequence to use than one from the <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/9642201">original paper</a> that used this marker? (All Perl scripts used below and data files produced are included in the PhotobiontDiversity <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity">repository</a>)</span>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">I have a local copy of the nt database that was current as of 17 April and I have configured the $</span><span style="line-height:1.5;">BLASTDB environmental variable so that I can just specify the database name. I use custom tabular formatting that includes the lengths of the query and hit sequences:</span>

<em>blastn -task megablast -query Ncommune_rbcX.fa -db nt -evalue 1e-20 -outfmt '6 qseqid qlen sacc slen pident length mismatch gapopen qstart qend qframe sstart send sframe evalue bitscore'  -out Nostoc_rbcX.bl</em>

That returns 500 sequences, which is quite a few less than I get if I search for "Nostoc rbcX" in the NCBI nucleotide database (595).

Get the sequences:

<em style="color:#444444;line-height:1.5;"> cut -f3 Nostoc_rbcX.bl |sort |uniq &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_acc.txt</em>

<em style="color:#444444;line-height:1.5;">blastdbcmd -db nt -entry_batch Nostoc_rbcX_acc.txt -out Nostoc_rbcX.fa</em>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Check if there are any extremely long sequences in the output that will cause the alignment program to choke:</span>

<em><span style="line-height:1.5;">awk '{if ($4 &gt; 1100) print $0; }' Nostoc_rbcX.bl </span></em>
<blockquote>gi|2463296|emb|Z94892.1| 964 KC291407 2349 93.84 795 24 14 1 770 1 1179 1973 1 0.0 1173
gi|2463296|emb|Z94892.1| 964 CP003642 7003560 87.16 771 90 4 1 770 1 2391377 2390615 1 0.0 867
gi|2463296|emb|Z94892.1| 964 CP001037 8234322 96.72 488 16 0 283 770 1 5265137 5265624 1 0.0 813
gi|2463296|emb|Z94892.1| 964 CP001037 8234322 97.17 283 8 0 1 283 1 5264778 5265060 1 2e-131 479</blockquote>
There are three sequences over 1100bp. One includes a full-length rbcL sequence, which isn't going to cause too many problems. The other two are chromosome-sized and will need to be trimmed down:

<em>FilterSeq.pl -i Nostoc_rbcX.fa -max 5000 -o Nostoc_rbcX_filtered.fa</em>

<em>GetSeq.pl Nostoc_rbcX.fa CP003642 | trunc.pl 2391377 2390615 &gt;&gt;Nostoc_rbcX_filtered.fa </em>

<em>GetSeq.pl Nostoc_rbcX.fa CP001037 |trunc.pl 5264778 5265624 &gt;&gt;Nostoc_rbcX_filtered.fa </em>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Reverse-complement negative-strand hits:</span>

<em>./RevCom.pl Nostoc_rbcX_filtered.fa &lt; Nostoc_rbcX.bl &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_revcom.fa</em>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Align the sequences with MAFFT:</span>

<em>mafft Nostoc_rbcX_revcom.fa &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_aln.fa</em>

Remove redundant sequences and trim ambiguous regions (this was done using MetaPIGA and saved as Nostoc_rbcX_trimmed.nex).

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Replace <strong>loooong</strong> NCBI names with accession numbers:</span>

<em><span style="line-height:1.5;">perl -pi -e 's/^\s*gi\|\d+\|\w+\|(\w+)\.\d\|\S+/$1/' Nostoc_rbcX_trim.nex </span></em>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Convert to Phylip format</span>

<em>ConvertSeq.pl -i Nostoc_rbcX_trimmed.nex -f phyext -o Nostoc_rbcX.phy</em>

Run phylogenetic analysis using PhyML:

<em>phyml -i Nostoc_rbcX.phy</em>

The resulting tree was visualised using FigTree. The most divergent sequences belong to the genus <em>Trichormus</em> and can safely be removed from the dataset. Another sequence belonging to <em>Cylindrospermum</em> is much less diverged and will be used as an outgroup to root the <em>Nostoc</em> tree.

Rerun phyml:

<em>phyml -i Nostoc_rbcX.phy</em>

This produces a <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Nostoc_rbcX.pdf?raw=true">reasonable-looking tree</a>. All of the early-branching <em>Nostoc</em> strains are from cultures and presumably belong to different species from the <em>N. commune/N. punctiforme</em> lineage that participates in all known symbioses with lichens and plants. The latter lineage includes a large number of relatively closely related strains, except for one lineage that appears to be evolving much more rapidly. I checked the alignment for these taxa and there are no obvious problems with it, but this is something I will have to look into in more detail in the future.

This tree isn't to interesting otherwise because there is no information about the host/ecology of any of the strains. Parsing out this information and displaying it will be the subject of my next post.]]></content:encoded>

  <title>Adding host information to the Nostoc phylogeny</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/04/29/adding-host-information/</link>
  <pubDate>Mon, 29 Apr 2013 15:21:48 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=54</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[<em></em>Having obtained 496 <em>Nostoc</em> rbcX sequences (plus one outgroup) and used them to infer a reasonable phylogeny, all that is left is to assign host association information to the branches. This will require (a) parsing the sequence files to obtain host info for each sequence, (b) associating each non-redundant sequence in the tree with the host info for all identical sequence, and (c) displaying all of this information on the tree.

The first step turned out to be surprisingly straightforward. The sequences are so consistently named that I was able to apply a "<a href="http://xkcd.com/1171/">simple</a>" regular expression to extract the host info from the fasta headers of the sequences:

<em>./ParseHost.pl &lt; Nostoc_rbcX.fa &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt</em>

This extracted the host genus and species names for all 459 lichen photobionts obtained from direct sequencing (though I had to edit a few of them to remove some extraneous information). The remaining 38 sequences were obtained from cultures (or directly from cyanobacterial colonies) and do not follow the same naming convention, even though a number of the cultures were derived from photobionts. Fortunately, most of these are from <a href="http://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09670260500342647">a single paper</a>, so it is fairly easy to extract the info from the tables in the paper.

The next problem is adding multiple host names to the branches representing redundant sequence group. I copied the info about redundant sequences from MetaPIGA into the file <em>Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt. </em>The names in this file are unwieldy because they contain the entire fasta header for each sequence. The first thing I will do is remove everything but the accession number from these names. This is a bit tricky because there are multiple names per line:

<em> perl -p -e 's/gi\|/|/' Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt | perl -p -e 's/\|\d+\|\w+\|(\w+)\.\d\|[^\|\n]+/$1, /g' &gt; temp.txt</em>

<em>mv temp.txt Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt</em>

I will next add this group information to the metadata table:

<em>cat Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt | ./AddGroup.pl Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt Nostoc_rbcX.nwk &gt;temp.txt</em>

<em>mv temp.txt Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt</em>

This script also outputs information about if each sequence is present in the tree so that I can tell which sequence represents each group. MetaPIGA assigns short sequences that match multiple different longer sequences to multiple groups. I assigned these ambiguous sequences to the most common matching sequence type.

Next, I add the host info to the tree for the unique sequences. The simplest way I could figure out to do this is to replace the accession numbers in the tree file with the host info. When multiple sequences from the same host species are identical, the total number is given in brackets after the host name. When multiple species from the same genus have identical sequences, the genus name is abbreviated after the first species.

<em> cat Nostoc_rbcX.nwk | ./AddHost.pl Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_host.nwk</em>

The resultant tree can be seen <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/130429/Nostoc_rbcX_host.pdf?raw=true">here</a>. The table with host information about each sequence can be seen <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt">here</a>. I will talk about the patterns revealed by this analysis in my next post.]]></content:encoded>

  <title>Adding new sequences (Mistakes were made)</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/02/adding-new-sequences/</link>
  <pubDate>Thu, 02 May 2013 12:48:52 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=77</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Looking through the tree produced in my last post, I noticed that several interesting sequences were missing from the tree. <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/04/25/obtaining-the-sequences/">There are also less sequences in the tree than I get if I search for "Nostoc rbcX" in Entrez</a>. Turns out that this is because blast+ limits the number of results returned to 500 by default. In retrospect, the fact that I ended up with <strong>exactly</strong> 500 sequences should have been a red flag. Fortunately, blast+ includes an option to exclude sequences from the results by GI number.

First I will move all of the old files to a separate folder and make a new folder for the new files:

<em>mkdir 130429 130502</em>

<em>mv Nostoc_rbcX* 130429/</em>

Extract GIs from sequence headers and write to a file:

<em>grep "&gt;" 130429/Nostoc_rbcX.fa | perl -p -e 's/&gt;gi\|(\d+)\|.*/$1/' &gt;130429/Nostoc_rbcX_gis.txt</em>

Repeat blast search using the -negative_gilist option:

<em>blastn -task megablast -query </em><em>Ncommune_rbcX.fa -db nt -evalue 1e-20 -outfmt '6 qseqid qlen sacc slen pident length mismatch gapopen qstart qend qframe sstart send sframe evalue bitscore' -negative_gilist 130429/Nostoc_rbcX_gis.txt -out 130502/Nostoc_rbcX.bl</em>

This returns 430 additional results, but the sequence similarity starts to drop off rather quickly, with only 19 sequences with an Evalue of less than 1e-180. The top hits below this value are all <em>Anabaena</em> sequences, so it is probably safe to use this as a cutoff for now.

Repeat blast search with a more stringent evalue cutoff:

<em>blastn -task megablast -query </em><em>Ncommune_rbcX.fa -db nt -evalue 1e-180 -outfmt '6 qseqid qlen sacc slen pident length mismatch gapopen qstart qend qframe sstart send sframe evalue bitscore' -negative_gilist 130429/Nostoc_rbcX_gis.txt -out 130502/Nostoc_rbcX.bl</em>

Obtaining the sequences, trimming, reverse-complementing, and renaming proceeds as described <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/04/25/obtaining-the-sequences/">previously</a>.

Before adding the new sequences to the alignment, it will be helpful to remove the redundant sequences so that the groupings do have to be recalculated in MetaPIGA:

<em>cat 130429/Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt | Scripts/RemoveRedundant.pl 130429/Nostoc_rbcX_aln.fa &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_nr_aln.fa</em>

Add new sequences to the alignment:

<em> mafft --add 130502/Nostoc_rbcX_filtered.fa Nostoc_rbcX_nr_aln.fa &gt; Nostoc_rbcX_aln.fa</em>

After applying automated filtering in MetaPIGA and building a tree as described previously, I got a tree that is very different from the one produced last time. Comparing the alignment files from the two analyses, 288 ambiguously aligned positions that were trimmed previously were left in this time. MetaPIGA uses the "gappyout" method from <a href="http://trimal.cgenomics.org/">TrimA</a>l for automated alignment trimming. This method appears to be highly sensitive to which sequences are included. In order to keep things consistent when I add new taxa, I am going to manually apply an exclusion set. Because the coordinates of the exclusion set may change as the dimensions of the alignment change, I selected a sequence without any insertions to act a reference. I then used the gap positions in this sequence as an exclusion set for the "-select" method in the command line version of TrimAl.

Remove redundant sequences:

Open <em>Nostoc_rbcX_aln.fa</em> in MetaPIGA, then select <em>Dataset-&gt;"Check for Ambiguous sequences."</em> Copy resultant groupings to <em>130502/Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt</em> and save the modified dataset as <em>Nostoc_rbcX.nex</em>

Trim ambiguous regions:

<em>Scripts/GetExcluded.pl Nostoc_rbcX_aln.fa |pbcopy</em>

<em>trimal -in Nostoc_rbcX.nex -phylip -select `pbpaste`  &gt;Nostoc_rbcX.phy</em>

This uses the very cool <a href="http://www.macresearch.org/using_pbcoby_and_pbpaste_to_bridge_the_terminal_and_the_desktop">pbcopy/pbcopy commands</a> to pipe output from the first command to the clipboard so it can be pasted into the command line for the second command. These commands are OSX specific, but there are similar commands available in <a href="https://coderwall.com/p/kdoqkq">other flavours of unix</a>.

Build tree:

<em>phyml -i Nostoc_rbcX.phy</em>

<em>mv Nostoc_rbcX.phy_phyml_tree.txt Nostoc_rbcX.nwk</em>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Next, I need to integrate the new groups of redundant sequences with the old set. This information also needs to be added to the Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt file. I started by parsing the fasta headers as previously to extract host information, then modified the AddGroups.pl script to compare the new list of redundant sequence groups to the previous list and to add new sequences to the old groups if necessary:</span>

Scripts/<em>ParseHost.pl &lt; 130502/Nostoc_rbcX.fa &gt; 130502/Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt</em>

<em> cat 130429/Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt 130502/Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt | Scripts/AddGroup.pl Nostoc_rbcX.nwk 130502/Nostoc_rbcX_groups.txt &gt; Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt</em>

Lastly, I added the host info from the metadata file to the tree and visualize with FigTree:

<em>Scripts/AddHost.pl Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt &lt; Nostoc_rbcX.nwk &gt;Nostoc_rbcX_host.nwk</em>

Which produces this <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/130502/Nostoc_rbcX.pdf?raw=true">tree</a>. Because this is a more conservative exclusion set, the tree looks quite different from the one I produced <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/130429/Nostoc_rbcX_host.pdf?raw=true">previously</a>, but if I reanalyse that dataset using this exclusion set, I get a <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/130502/Nostoc_rbcX_old.pdf?raw=true">very similar topology</a>.

A few quick observations about this tree for now:

The most basal lichen photobiont sequence (DQ185264 from <em>Peltigera didactyla</em>) is from a culture and may represent an epiphyte or accessory symbiont, rather than the true photobiont.

The next branch includes all sequenced photobionts of <em>Peltigera malacea</em>, <em>Leptogium lichenoides</em> and <em>Sticta hypochra</em>, each of which forms a more-or-less distinct cluster:

<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/specific_group.png"><img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-129" alt="specific_group" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/specific_group.png?w=300" width="300" height="169" /></a>

The much-discussed <a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/content/297/5580/357.short"><em>Nephroma</em> guild</a> is not recovered as monophyletic but forms a paraphyletic grade at the base of the core photobiont lineage (I suspect that this may be an artifact of some kind):

<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/nephroma_guild.png"><img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-130" alt="Nephroma_guild" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/nephroma_guild.png?w=300" width="300" height="260" /></a>

There is one <em>Stereocaulon</em> photobiont, which is on an extremely long branch, that groups with four divergent <em>Peltigera neopolydactyla</em> photobionts, though there are other <em>P. neopolydactyla</em> photobionts throughout the tree:

<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/stereocaulon.png"><img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-131" alt="Stereocaulon" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/stereocaulon.png?w=300" width="300" height="204" /></a>

There are plenty of other interesting observations to be made about this tree. More soon.]]></content:encoded>

  <title>An In-Depth Look at the Diversity of Symbiotic Nostoc</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/08/an-in-depth-look-at-the-diversity-of-symbiotic-nostoc/</link>
  <pubDate>Wed, 08 May 2013 12:40:27 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=136</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[<strong>**Post has been updated with some corrections to the host information in the first phylogeny**</strong>

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Today I am finally going to take a detailed look at the </span><em style="color:#444444;line-height:1.5;">Nostoc</em><span style="line-height:1.5;"> phylogeny that I have been working on. But before I can begin, I have to figure out a way to highlight interesting taxa in an automated way. To do this, I wrote a script that adds html color tags after taxon names according to various classifications. While I was at it, I converted the branch support values to a binary system (≥0.9 vs. &lt;0.9), which I can display as black circles on significantly supported branches. Note that this script requires that the tree be in NEXUS  format rather than the plain Newick that is produced by PhyML. Opening the tree file in FigTree and saving it converts it to NEXUS, or the conversion could be scripted using </span><a style="line-height:1.5;" href="http://www.bioperl.org/wiki/Main_Page">Bioperl</a><span style="line-height:1.5;">.</span>

First, lets compare lichen photobionts to other free-living and symbiotic <em>Nostoc</em> strains:

<em>../Scripts/ColourTree.pl ../Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt ../Nostoc_rbcX_host.nwk host &gt;host_tree.nwk</em>

[caption id="attachment_157" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/host_tree3.jpg"><img class="size-large wp-image-157" alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by type of association (purple: lichen photobionts, green: plant symbionts, blue: free-living, red: fungal endosymbiont). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/host_tree3.jpg?w=640" width="640" height="636" /></a> Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by type of association (purple: lichen photobionts, green: plant symbionts, blue: free-living, red: fungal endosymbiont). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

As mentioned last time, the earliest branching taxa are free-living <em>Nostoc</em> isolates, along with a culture isolated from <em>Peltigera</em>, which I suspect may not be a true photobiont. There are also other free-living strains throughout the rest of the phylogeny that have been identified as <em>N. edaphicum, N. calcicola, N. commune, N. muscorum and N. flagelliforme</em>. Cyanobacterial taxonomy is a mess, but that is a topic for another day. There are also symbionts from a variety of plant groups throughout the main crown group including Cycads (Cycas, <em>Macrozamia</em> and <em>Encephalartos</em>), Bryophytes (<em>Blassia</em> and <em>Anthoceros</em>) and the angiosperm <em>Gunnera</em>. There are two cases where lichen photobionts are identical to plant symbionts (coloured black in the tree). Finally, there are two symbionts from <em><a href="http://schuessler.userweb.mwn.de/geosiphon/geosiphon_home.html">Geosiphon pyriforme</a></em>, a weird unicellular primative fungus, that hosts intracellular symbionts in sac-like, multinucleate cells (coloured red). There is some debate as to whether this symbiosis should be classified as a lichen or not.

Next, we can look at photobionts of different lichen families (the taxonomy of the lichen is based on that of the fungal partner):

<em>../Scripts/ColourTree.pl ../Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt ../Nostoc_rbcX_host.nwk family &gt;family_tree.nwk</em>

[caption id="attachment_150" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/family_tree1.jpg"><img class="size-large wp-image-150" alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host family (purple: Stereocaulaceae, green: Lobariaceae, blue: Peltigerales, red: Collemetaceae, yellow: Nephromataceae, brown: Pannariaceae). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Names in grey indicate non-lichenized strains. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/family_tree1.jpg?w=640" width="640" height="489" /></a> Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host family (purple: Stereocaulaceae, green: Lobariaceae, blue: Peltigerales, red: Collemetaceae, yellow: Nephromataceae, brown: Pannariaceae). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group or photobionts of lichens with uncertain taxonomic position (<em>Massalongia</em>). Names in grey indicate non-lichenized strains. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

<span style="line-height:1.5;">At the deepest nodes in the tree, there is clearly a lot of host switching between different lichen families, but there is a lot of clustering of photobionts from the same lichen family at the tips of the tree. Photobionts of lichens in the Lobariaceae, Nephromataceae and Pannariaceae are all mixed up, which has been </span><a style="line-height:1.5;" href="https://www.sciencemag.org/content/297/5580/357.full">noted previously</a><span style="line-height:1.5;"> and has been proposed to reflect the ecological similarities of the hosts. There also appears to be a lot of historic photobiont sharing between lichens in the Peltigeraceae and the Collmenataceae, but such sharing is not ongoing as in all cases there are long branches separating photobionts of these families. <em>Stereocaulon</em> is the only species represented in the tree that is not part of the Peltigerales, an order of lichens that are universally associated with <em>Nostoc</em>, either as the sole photosynthetic partner or as a secondary photobiont. It would be interesting to see if other non-Peltigeralean lichens also associate with such divergent <em>Nostoc</em> genotypes.</span>

Lastly, let's take a look at species-level patterns. There is a lot of host switching among members of the same genus, but there do appear to be some species that are highly specialised:

<em>../Scripts/ColourTree.pl ../Nostoc_rbcX_metadata.txt ../Nostoc_rbcX_host.nwk specialists &gt;specialist_tree.nwk</em>

[caption id="attachment_151" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/specialist_tree.jpg"><img class="size-large wp-image-151" alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host species Names in grey indicate non-specialist hosts. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/specialist_tree.jpg?w=640" width="640" height="486" /></a> Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host species Names in grey indicate non-specialist hosts. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

With the current sampling, it is possible to identify four species of <em>Leptogium</em> and one each of <em>Collema, Peltigera and Sticta</em> that exclusively associate with a single cluster of photobionts, which is, in turn, exclusively associated with that lichen species (<a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1055790310002320">reciprocal specificity</a>). Note that there is one <em>P. malacea </em>photobiont that falls out of the <em>P. malacea </em>cluster, but there are about four times as many specimens of this species as there are for any of the other specialists. As <a href="https://www.sciencemag.org/content/297/5580/357.full">noted previously</a>, these specialists predominate in the basal symbiotic <em>Nostoc</em> lineage.

There is a lot more that could be said about this tree, but I think I'll leave it there for now. See<a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/nph.12165/full"> this paper</a> for a more detailed analysis of the complex photobiont specialisation patterns in <em>Peltigera</em>, including geographic patterns. On to the green algal photobionts in my next post...

  <title>Green Algal Photobionts: Trebouxia</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/16/green-algal-photobionts-trebouxia/</link>
  <pubDate>Thu, 16 May 2013 09:45:37 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=159</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Having beaten the phylogeny of symbiotic cyanobacteria into submission in my <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/08/an-in-depth-look-at-the-diversity-of-symbiotic-nostoc/">previous post</a>, I am now tackling the green algae. My plan was to start with a big-picture analysis of 18S ribosomal RNA sequences, but my initial blast search returned over 10,00o 454 reads from metagenomic projects which was a lot more "environmental isolate XXX" than I felt like dealing with. Besides, I don't know that I could add much to <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232904551_Revisiting_photobiont_diversity_in_the_lichen_family_Verrucariaceae_(Ascomycota)">this</a> recent overview. Therefore, I am going to focus on the most important lineage of lichenized algae: <em>Trebouxia</em>. There have been a large number of studies that have obtained photobiont ITS sequences from a variety of <em>Trebouxia</em> associated lichens, so these are the data that I looked at.

The methods are the same as the ones that I described in detail <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/tag/methods/">previously</a> for <i>Nostoc </i>ITS sequences. Briefly, I used two ITS sequences (<em>T. impressa</em> JN204819 and <em>T. arboricola</em> JQ993781) as queries to identify all homologous (E-value &lt;= 1e-100) sequences in the nt database. Sequences were aligned with MAFFT, duplicate sequences were removed with MetaPIGA, alignment positions corresponding to gaps in the references sequence (<em>T. arboricola</em> JQ993758) were removed with trimal, and phylogenetic relationships were inferred with PhyML.

This procedure produced a tree with 794 taxa representing 1840 <em>Trebouxia</em> ITS sequences. The actual number of <em>Trebouxia</em> associated lichens that have been sequenced is much higher than this because many authors only deposit representative sequences of each haplotype that they obtained. At some point I will dig into the papers where this has been done to extract the real numbers, but I have not done so yet.

For now, I am going to focus on the taxonomy of the algae. I will leave a discussion of the host-association patterns for a future post. Here is the Trebouxia ITS phylogeny color-coded by species (tree file can be found <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130516/Trebouxia_ITS_species.nwk">here</a>):

[caption id="attachment_161" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/trebouxia.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-161 " alt="Trebouxia ITS phylogeny" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/05/trebouxia.jpg?w=640" width="640" height="456" /></a> Trebouxia ITS phylogeny color-coded by species (dark green: T. jamesii, yellow: T. corticola, light green: T. incrustata, brown: T. asymmetrica, ornage: T. gigantea, purple: T. gelatinosa, dark blue: T. impressa, light red: T. arboricola, light blue: T. decolorans, dark red: other, grey: T. sp.). Sequences recovered from multiple named species are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt; 0.9[/caption]

With a few exceptions, sequences from named algae tend to cluster very well. <em>T. gelatinosa</em> (purple) is nested within<em> T. impressa</em> (dark blue), though given the long branch separating these two species from all of the others, I don't entirely trust the rooting of this clade. <em>T. jamesii</em> (dark green) is a very heterogeneous group as has been recognised <a href="http://taylorlab.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/taylorlab/publications/kroken2000b.pdf">previously</a>. A number of photobionts that group with <em>T. decolorans</em> (light blue) have been identified as <em>T. arboricola</em> (light red). Three major lineages have no named members (except for some presumably misidentified <em>T. decolorans</em> sequences).

In addition to the differentially coloured species, there are several additional species names that are represented by a small number of sequences, all of which are colored dark red in the tree. <em>T. australis, T. brindabellae, T. showmanii </em>and<em> T. usneae</em> are each found in distinct clusters and are likely to represent additional good species. <em>T. australis </em>and<em> T. brindabellae</em> are both in clusters near the base of one of the <em>T. jamesii</em> clades (dark green). Two <em>T. showmanii</em> sequences form the sister group to <em>T. incrustata</em> (light green). <em>T. usneae</em> forms a distint lineage with a misidentified <em>T. corticola</em> sequence sister to the <em>T. corticola</em> lineage (yellow). All other rare species are deeply nested within other common species and appear not to be distinct. These include <em>T. potteri</em> which is nested within T. impressa (dark blue), <em>T. aggregata</em> and <em>T. crenulata</em> which are nested within <em>T. arboricola</em> (light red) and T. simplex, which includes six sequences that are identical to <em>T. jamesii</em> (black) and two other sequences that are nested within one of the <em>T. jamesii</em> clades (dark green). <em>T. flava</em> is identical to a <em>T. impressa</em> sequence and is coloured black in the tree.

In conclusion, &gt;1840 <em>Trebouxia</em> ITS sequences that have been obtained from lichens cluster into about 24 distinct species, 13 of which appear to have suitable named representatives in the database. Two of the <em>T. jamesii</em> clusters have been given the provisional names <em>T. "vulpinea"</em> and <em>T. "letharii"</em> but it looks like at least three additional names are needed for this group.

That's it for now. In my next post I will map host information onto this tree.

<item>
  <title>A preliminary look at host association patterns in Trebouxia</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/06/05/a-preliminary-look-at-trebouxia-host-associations/</link>
  <pubDate>Wed, 05 Jun 2013 10:33:23 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=186</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Having gone through the steps to build a phylogeny of the most common lichen photobiont, <em>Trebouxia</em> in my<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/16/green-algal-photobionts-trebouxia/"> last post</a>, I will now go on to discussing the host association patterns that it reveals. Here is the <em>Trebouxia</em> ITS tree generated previously:

[caption id="attachment_210" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/trebouxia_its1.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-210" alt="Trebouxia ITS phylogeny. Major clades are differentilly coloured and named according to authentic strains" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/trebouxia_its1.jpg" width="640" height="480" /></a> Trebouxia ITS phylogeny. Major clades are differentilly coloured and named according to authentic strains[/caption]

I've coloured all of the taxa within clades according to the colours of the named strains and I've assigned unique colours to each clade that does not contain named strains. I have not attempted to break up<em> T. jamesii </em>or<em> T. impressa</em> into sub-clades, though doing so would probably be justified. This will be a topic for a future post. I should also point out that <em>T. jamesi</em>i is referred to as <em>T. simplex</em> is <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/16332336">some</a> <a href="http://www.uni-graz.at/~grubem/pub/Blaha06.pdf">papers</a>.

In contrast to <em>Nostoc</em> photobionts where the fasta headers were consistently labeled with the host information, these sequences are ...not. I used a bioperl wrapper to NCBI's Eutils interface to download genbank format sequences and parsed them to extract host association information from the "host", "note" and "isolation source" annotions. I also extracted information about the author of each sequence and where it was published while I was at it:

<em>../Scripts/GetGB.pl Trebouxia_ITS_acc.txt heath.obrien-at-gmail-dot-com &gt; Trebouxia_ITS.gbk</em>

<em>../Scripts/ParseHost.pl Trebouxia_ITS.gbk &gt; host_info.txt</em>

<em>../Scripts/GetRef.py Trebouxia_ITS.gbk &gt;ref_info.txt</em>

This information was added to Trebouxia_ITS_metadata.txt in Excel and missing values were filled in manually where possible. I also added culture collection numbers where available and started to fill in locality information, but I haven't gotten very far with the latter.

Next, I added information about which clade each sequence fell into to the <em>Trebouxia</em> metadata file. The <a href="http://ete.cgenomics.org/">ETE</a> python toolkit was invaluable for this step and was my main proximate motivation for switching from perl to python for my scripting, but I was also really, REALLY tired of having to keep my '\@'s and '%{$'s straight:

<em>../Script/GetClades.py -t Trebouxia_ITS.nwk -m Trebouxia_ITS_metadata.txt &gt;temp</em>

<em>mv temp Trebouxia_ITS_metadata.txt</em>

In this case, the information was added to the metadata file automatically.

Lastly, I wrote a script to count the number of times that <em>Trebouxia</em> from each clade was associated with each lichen genus that has been sampled:

<em>../Scripts/CountAssociations.py -m Trebouxia_ITS_metadata.txt &gt; AssocaitionCounts.txt</em>

After some fiddling with conditional formatting in Excel, these analyses produced this:

[caption id="attachment_214" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/screen-shot-2013-06-05-at-11-00-21.png"><img class=" wp-image-214" alt="Trebouxia_associations.png" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/screen-shot-2013-06-05-at-11-00-21.png" width="640" height="470" /></a> Counts of associations between Trebouxia clades and lichen genera. Colour coding matches the phylogeny[/caption]

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Before discussing the patterns, I should point out that these counts are of how many sequence records have been deposited in genbank, not the number os specimens that have been sampled as many authors deposit representative haplotype sequences rather than all of their data. Incorporating this information will change the counts dramatically in some cases.</span>

The most extensively sampled genus is <em>Letharia</em>, which is exclusively associated with <em>Trebouxia jamesii</em>. In fact, there <a href="http://taylorlab.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/taylorlab/publications/kroken2000b.pdf">appears</a> to be strong reciprocal specificity acting between species of <em>Letharia</em> and subclades within <em>T. jamesii</em>, a topic I would like to explore further in the future.

Eight other genera in the Parmeliaceae are also exclusively associated with <em>T. jamesii</em> photobionts, including <em>Cetraria</em> (76 sequences), <em>Evernia</em> (19 sequences), <em>Flavocetraria</em> (17 sequences), <em>Hypogymnia</em> (12 sequences) and <em>Pseudevernia</em> (10 sequences). However, <em>Parmelia</em> (10 sequences), <em>Flavoparmelia</em> (6 sequences), and 4 other genera associate with photobionts in the <em>T. impressa</em>/<em>T. gelatinosa</em> clade (among others) and <em>Parmotrema</em> is highly specific for <em>T. corticola</em> photobionts, with 135 <em>P. tinctorum</em> photobionts grouping with <em>T. corticola</em> (the <em>Trebouxia</em> sp. 3 photobiont is from a different <em>Parmotrema</em> species). The <em>P. tinctorum</em> / <em>T. corticola</em> association is another case of reciprocal specificity as 135 of 141 <em>T. corticola</em> sequences are from <em>P. tinctorum</em> photoboints.

All photobionts from <em>Lasallia</em> (28 sequences) and most from <em>Umbilicaria</em> (105 of 131 sequences) also grouped with <em>T. jamesii</em>, with most of the exceptions being specimens collected in the <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22671139">Antarctic</a> (see also <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12140232">this</a> paper). <em>T. jamesii</em> was also the predominant photobiont of <em>Thamnolia</em> (22 out of 28 sequences) and <em>Chaenotheca</em> (7 of 8 sequences). <em>T. jamesii</em> was also a common photobiont of <em>Lecanora</em> and <em>Lecidea</em>, but p<span style="line-height:1.5;">hotobiont diversity in both of these genera, and the Lecaonraceae in general, is extremely high, with  <em>Lecanora</em> photobionts falling into 11 </span><span style="line-height:1.5;">different species and <em>Lecidea</em> photobionts falling into 8. </span>Indeed, 6 species of photobiont <a href="http://www.uni-graz.at/~grubem/pub/Blaha06.pdf">have been recovered from <em>L. rupicola</em> alone</a>. Lichens in the Lecanoraceae are the hosts for the vast majority of <em>T. asymmetrica, T. incrustata, T. showmanii </em>and<em> T</em>. sp. 1 photobiots that have been sampled.

Similar to the Parmeliaceae, most of the genera in the Physciaceae are specific for the same <em>Trebouxia</em> lineage, while other genera do not associate with it at all. <em>Physconia</em> (40 sequences) and <em>Phaeophyscia</em> (7 sequences) are exclusively associated with <em>T. impressa</em>, as are 29 out of 36 <em>Physcia</em> sequences while only 2 of 5 <em>Rinodina</em> photobionts and none from <em>Anaptychia</em> (8 sequences) group with <em>T. impressa</em>.

<span style="line-height:1.5;">All but 9 of 133 <em>Xanthoria</em> photobionts belong to <em>T. decolorans</em> or <em>T. arboricola</em>, which are  sister species. These photobionts also predominate in most of the other genera in the  Teloschistaceae, including 53 of 65 <em>Caloplaca</em> photobionts, 4 of 4 </span><em>Huea</em> photobionts, and 34 of 68 <em>Tephromela</em> photobionts, a genus that is also frequently associated with <em>Trebouxia</em> sp. 2 photobionts.

<span style="line-height:1.5;">Specificity is also high for </span><em style="line-height:1.5;color:#444444;">Ramalina</em><span style="line-height:1.5;">, with 139 of 150 photobiont sequences restricted to two </span><em style="line-height:1.5;color:#444444;">Trebouxia</em><span style="line-height:1.5;"> clades. </span><em style="line-height:1.5;color:#444444;">Boreoplaca</em><span style="line-height:1.5;"> is associated with one of the same photobionts (<em>Trebouxia</em> sp. 2), but not with the most common </span><em style="line-height:1.5;color:#444444;">Ramalina</em><span style="line-height:1.5;"> photobiont (</span><em style="line-height:1.5;color:#444444;">T. decolorans</em><span style="line-height:1.5;">; 116 of 150 sequences).</span>

Thus, there is a wide range of association patterns, from extreme reciprocal specificity (135 of 141 <em>T. corticola</em> sequences associated with <em>Parmotrema</em> and all <em>P. tinctorum</em> specimens associated with <em>T. corticola</em>) to generalism (<em>Lecanora</em> rupicola associating with  6 different <em>Trebouxia</em> species). There is some evidence of phylogenetic inertia, as lichen genera from the same family are more likely to share similar photobiont association patterns than unrelated lichens, but there is also a lot of plasticity. There are a lot of ideas out there about the ecological and life history factors that could be causing these differences, but given the complexity of the patterns and our lack of knowledge of things like lichen demography and dispersal mechanisms, it will probably be some time before definitive explanations can be provided.

<item>
  <title>Green Algal Photobionts: Asterochloris.</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/06/12/green-algal-photobionts-asterochloris/</link>
  <pubDate>Wed, 12 Jun 2013 14:49:14 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=225</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[In this post I will be taking a look at the diversity of the junior partner to <em>Trebouxia</em>: <em><a href="http://botany.natur.cuni.cz/skaloud/confocal/astero.html">Asterochloris</a></em>. Originally described by <a href="http://81.10.184.26:9001/personen_add/Tschermak-Woess_Elisabeth_1.PDF">Elisabeth Tschermak-Woess</a> in 1980, <em>Asterochloris</em> was subsequently merged with <em>Trebouxia</em> before being split out again on the basis of sequence data in the late 1990s. For the most part, <em>Asterochloris</em> is thought to be restricted to associations with lichens in two closely related families, Cladoniaceae and Stereocaulaceae. However, this includes <em>Cladonia</em>, one of the more <a href="http://lichens.digitalmycology.com/macrolichens/Cladonia.html">charismatic</a> (and <a href="http://www.blueplanetbiomes.org/caribou_moss.htm">ecologically important</a>) lichen groups, so <em>Asterochloris</em> has been extensively sampled.

Sequences were obtained and analysed as described <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/02/adding-new-sequences/">previously</a>, except that I decided to use a command-line application to remove redundant sequences instead of the GUI-based program MetaPIGA. In addition to being easier to automate, this has the advantage of being much better <a href="http://drive5.com/usearch/manual/uclust_algo.html">documented</a> and WAY faster:

<em>usearch -cluster_fast Asterochloris_ITS.fa -id 1 -centroids Asterochloris_ITS_nr.fa -uc Asterochloris_ITS_groups.txt</em>

This works on unaligned sequences and produces a file of non-redundant sequences in addition to the list of groups, which can be used directly for alignment and phylogenetic inference. The detailed steps of this analysis are <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130612/log.txt">here</a>. Datasets can be found <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/tree/master/130612">here</a>.

The resulting phylogeny, colour-coded by algal species names looks like this:

[caption id="attachment_233" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/asterochloris_its_species.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-233" alt="Asterochloris ITS phylogeny color-coded by species (light blue: A. irregularis, red: A. glomerata, dark blue: A. phycobiontica, dark green: A. magna, ornage: A. excentrica, purple: A. italiana, light green: A. erici, grey: A. sp.). Sequences recovered from multiple names species are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;=  0.9" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/asterochloris_its_species.jpg" width="640" height="468" /></a> Asterochloris ITS phylogeny color-coded by species (light blue: A. irregularis, red: A. glomerata, dark blue: A. phycobiontica, dark green: A. magna, ornage: A. excentrica, purple: A. italiana, light green: A. erici, grey: A. sp.). Sequences recovered from multiple names species are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9[/caption]

The structure of the tree looks quite similar to that of <em><a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/06/05/a-preliminary-look-at-trebouxia-host-associations/">Trebouxia</a></em>, and <a href="http://images.algaebase.org/pdf/562E29E51dfba31B50XMY38408E5/47442.pdf">this</a> paper argues that many of these clusters represent distinct species. However, the sequence divergence within the genus is much, much lower than it is for <em>Trebouxia</em>:

[caption id="attachment_236" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/trebouxia_tree.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-236" alt="Trebouxia and Asterochloris ITS trees with branch lengths drawn to the same scale" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/trebouxia_tree.jpg" width="640" height="376" /></a> Trebouxia and Asterochloris ITS trees with branch lengths drawn to the same scale[/caption]

This has more to do with the huge amount of sequence divergence within <em>Trebouxia</em> than it does any lack of diversity in <em>Asterochloris</em>. Most of <em>Asterochloris</em> clusters above have at least 2% sequence divergence from one another, so it's not unreasonable to consider them different species.

The named representatives of three species (<em>A. phycobiontica</em> (dark blue), <em>A. erici</em> (light green) and <em>A. excentrica</em> (light blue) ) form coherent clusters in the tree. Strains identified as <em>A. glomerata</em> (red), <em>A. magna</em> (dark green) and<em> A. irregularis</em> (orange) each fall into two clusters, though it is difficult to see for the latter two species because some of the sequences are identical to those from representatives of other species and are thus labeled black. Two additional species, <em>A. italiana</em> and <em>A. pyriformis </em>are only represented by sequences that are identical to sequences from other species.

As for the host association patterns, the vast majority of isolates are from members of the genera <em>Cladonia</em> (blue), <em>Lepraria</em> (red) and <em>Stereocaulon</em> (purple):

[caption id="attachment_237" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/asterochloris_its_host.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-237" alt="Asterochloris ITS phylogeny color-coded by host genus ( dark blue: Cladonia, red: Lepraria, purple: Stereocaulon, light blue: Pilophorus, dark green: Anzina, light green: Varicellaria, orange: Diploschistes, grey: unknown/other). Sequences recovered from multiple names species are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;=  0.9" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/asterochloris_its_host.jpg" width="640" height="484" /></a> Asterochloris ITS phylogeny color-coded by host genus ( dark blue: Cladonia, red: Lepraria, purple: Stereocaulon, light blue: Pilophorus, dark green: Anzina, light green: Varicellaria, orange: Diploschistes, grey: unknown/other). Sequences recovered from multiple names species are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9[/caption]

There are also photobionts of <em>Cladia, <em>Pilophorus, Pycnothelia, </em><em>Anzina, </em>Diploschistes, Ochrolechia, Varicellaria </em>and<em> Xanthoria.</em> The first three of these are closely related to <em>Cladonia</em>, while the others are a diverse assemblage of lichens. With the exception of <em>Xanthoria</em>, none of these genera have been found to associate with <em>Trebouxia. Xanthoria </em>is listed as the host for<em> A. italiana</em> in GenBank, but the authors make no mention of this in their paper and the host is not listed in the <a href="http://www.ccap.ac.uk/strain_info.php?Strain_No=219/5B">culture collection info</a>, so it should probably be taken with a grain of salt.

Interestingly, there are also a number of <em>Asterochloris</em> sequences that were obtained from environmental sampling (one from <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=22571668">limestone rock</a> and two from <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/?term=19659501">forest soil</a> and ten from <a href="http://iopscience.iop.org/1748-9326/8/1/015033">a glacier forefield</a>). It is certainly possible that these were derived from lichen fragments or vegetative propagules, but there was only a single <em>Trebouxia</em> sequence that was not from a lichen thallus despite almost three times as much sampling, so these results suggest that <em>Asterochloris</em> may be a facultative lichen photobiont while <em>Trebouxia</em> is obligately lichenized.

Other than the <em>A. phycobiontica</em> cluster which is associated with a diverse array of lichen genera, <em>Lepraria</em> tends to be associated with distinct lineages compared to <em>Cladonia</em> and <em>Stereocaulon</em>, while the latter genera overlap in their photobiont preferences. There is also a lot of interesting host association patterns at the <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15487996">species level</a> for this group, a topic that I hope to explore in the future.

  <title>Gunnera symbionts do not cluster with lichen photobionts</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/06/20/gunnera-symbionts-do-not-cluster-with-lichen-photobionts/</link>
  <pubDate>Thu, 20 Jun 2013 16:03:21 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=250</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[I have been kicking around the idea of setting up an online catalog of lichen photobionts for years before I started doing this. The main impetus to finally start was this paper:

<span class="Z3988" title="ctx_ver=Z39.88-2004&amp;rft_val_fmt=info%3Aofi%2Ffmt%3Akev%3Amtx%3Ajournal&amp;rft.jtitle=Microbial+Ecology&amp;rft_id=info%3Adoi%2F10.1007%2Fs00248-013-0223-2&amp;rfr_id=info%3Asid%2Fresearchblogging.org&amp;rft.atitle=Diversity+of+Endosymbiotic+Nostoc+in+Gunnera+magellanica+%28L%29+from+Tierra+del+Fuego%2C+Chile&amp;rft.issn=0095-3628&amp;rft.date=2013&amp;rft.volume=&amp;rft.issue=&amp;rft.spage=&amp;rft.epage=&amp;rft.artnum=http%3A%2F%2Flink.springer.com%2F10.1007%2Fs00248-013-0223-2&amp;rft.au=Fern%C3%A1ndez-Mart%C3%ADnez%2C+M.&amp;rft.au=de+los+R%C3%ADos%2C+A.&amp;rft.au=Sancho%2C+L.&amp;rft.au=P%C3%A9rez-Ortega%2C+S.&amp;rfe_dat=bpr3.included=1;bpr3.tags=Biology">Fernández-Martínez, M., de los Ríos, A., Sancho, L., &amp; Pérez-Ortega, S. (2013). Diversity of Endosymbiotic <em>Nostoc</em> in <em>Gunnera magellanica</em> (L) from Tierra del Fuego, Chile <span style="font-style:italic;">Microbial Ecology</span> DOI: <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.1007/s00248-013-0223-2" rev="review">10.1007/s00248-013-0223-2</a></span>

I have been interested in the relationships between lichenized <em>Nostoc</em> and those that form symbioses with ferns, cycads, liverworts and the flowering plant <em>Gunnera</em> for a long time, but <a href="http://www.lutzonilab.net/publications/lutzoni_file323.pdf">my efforts</a> to address the issue were hampered by inadequate sampling and others who had <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1574-6941.1999.tb00563.x/abstract">better sampling</a> used a genetic marker with a <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12032241">complex history</a> that made such comparisons difficult. Finally, here was a paper with extensive sampling across the range of a plant host of <em>Nostoc</em> who used the same genetic marker that has been widely adopted in work on lichen photobionts. Unfortunately, while the paper has a lot of interesting things to say about the <em>Nostoc-Gunnera</em> symbiosis (genetically monomorphic within individuals, lots of variability among individuals, reduced symbiont diversity in recently deglaciated areas, etc), they included very few lichen photobionts in their analyses, so I wasn't able to get the answers to the questions I'm interested in from the paper.

Now that I've developed a <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/08/an-in-depth-look-at-the-diversity-of-symbiotic-nostoc/">decent phylogentic framework for symbiotic <em>Nostoc</em></a>, it should now be possible to address these questions. Here is how the G. <em>magellanica </em>symbionts fit in:

[caption id="attachment_257" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/nostoc_rbcx_host.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-257" alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny with Gunnera magellanica symbiont hilighted, coloured by type of association (purple: lichen photobionts, green: plant symbionts, blue: free-living, red: fungal endosymbiont). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/06/nostoc_rbcx_host.jpg" width="640" height="605" /></a> Nostoc rbcX phylogeny with Gunnera magellanica symbiont hilighted, coloured by type of association (purple: lichen photobionts, green: plant symbionts, blue: free-living, red: fungal endosymbiont). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

A few things about this tree are interesting. For one, <em>G. magellanica</em> symbionts form two well-supported lineages to exclusion of all other strains. This contradicts the results from the paper, where two <em>G. magellanica</em> symbiont haplotypes did not group with the others and where one lichen photobiont was nested within one of the <em>G. magellanica</em> symbiont clusters, though resolution and support were low for these nodes in their tree. Secondly, the <em>G. magellanica</em> symbionts do not group with any other plant symbionts, including the other <em>Gunnera</em> symbiont. Thirdly, the <em>G. magellanica</em> symbionts are on relatively long branches, suggesting that the evolutionary rate is higher in this lineage.

One major note of caution are in order when interpreting this tree, however: all of these <em>G. magellanica</em> symbionts were collected in the southern tip of South America, while the vast majority of the sampling of other lineages, including many of the plant symbionts, is from the northern hemisphere. Indeed, several of the symbionts of tropical plants were isolated from botanic gardens in Europe, well outside of the native range of the plants. It is certainly possible that lichens and other plant hosts from South America would associate with some of the same <em>Nostoc</em> strains isolated from <em>G. magellanica.</em>

So, in conclusion, it's fair to say that these data support the notion of frequent host shifts between lichens and plants in the evolutionary history of <em>Nostoc</em>, but that there is no evidence that the same strains of Nostoc routinely form symbionses with lichens and with <em>Gunnera</em>. However, more geographically appropriate sampling may provide this evidence in the future.

<strong>Methods</strong>:

Sequences KF142679 to KF142710 were downloaded from genbank and added to the <em>Nostoc</em> rbcX dataset. These 32 haplotype sequences represent 110 specimens, but it is not clear how many specimens each haplotype represents. Details of the analysis can be found <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130620/log.txt">here</a>. Data files are <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/tree/master/130620">here</a>.

  <title>Green Algal Photobionts: Coccomyxa</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/07/11/green-algal-photobionts-coccomyxa/</link>
  <pubDate>Thu, 11 Jul 2013 15:09:59 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=278</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[After a few weeks of for a vacation, it's time to get back to green algal photobionts. In addition to <em>Trebouxia</em> and <em>Asterochloris</em>, there are several other genera of Trebouxiophycean algae that act as photobionts for various groups of lichens. The most significant of these is probably <em>Coccomyxa</em>, which is the photobiont of many mushroom-forming <a href="http://www.anbg.gov.au/lichen/basidiolichens.html">basidiolichens</a> and is also the green algal component of tripartite lichens in the Peltigerales.

There hasn't been much work on <em>Coccomyxa</em>, but there were two papers in 2003 that each sequenced ITS from 20-25 specimens. <a href="http://www.lutzonilab.net/publications/lutzoni_file186.pdf">One study</a> found that photobionts of basidiolichens, photobionts of Peltigeralian lichens and free-living strains each formed a distinct lineage (Zoller et al. 2003), while <a href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0024282903000513">the other</a> found that photobionts of two species each of <em>Nephroma</em> and <em>Peltigera</em> were nearly identical (variable at a single position), which a single <em>Peltigera britannica</em> photobiont was found to be significantly different (Lohtander et al. 2003). <em>Coccomyxa</em> and <em>Pseudococcomyxa</em> have also been reported as symbionts of protists including <em>Paramecium</em> and <em>Stentor</em>.

Seventy-seven ITS sequences were obtained and analysed as described <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/02/adding-new-sequences/">previously</a>. Representative ITS sequences from Lohtander et al. were expanded based on the data in Table 2 of their paper. Details of the analyses are <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130711/log.txt">here</a>. Datasets can be found <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/tree/master/130711">here</a>. The resulting tree looks like this:

[caption id="attachment_286" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/coccomyxa_its_host.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-286" alt="Coccomyxa ITS phylogeny color-coded according to the clades identified by Zoller et al, 2003: blue, L/O, red, L/P, green, F. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/coccomyxa_its_host.jpg" width="640" height="700" /></a> Coccomyxa ITS phylogeny color-coded according to the clades identified by Zoller et al, 2003: blue, L/O, red, L/P, green, F. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9. Tree is mid-point rooted[/caption]

The clade coloured blue in the figure corresponds to the basidiolichen clade mentioned above. It also includes the outlier sequence from <em>P. britannica</em> found by Lohtander et al. The clade in red corresponds to the Peltigeralean photobiont clade above and includes all other <em>Peltigera</em> and <em>Nephroma</em> sequences obtained by Lohtander et al. The clade in green corresponds to the free-living clade. This free-living clade also includes two symbionts of <em>Paramecium bursaria</em> while two other <em>P. bursaria</em> symbionts and one from <em>Stentor amethystinus</em> fall outside of these three main clades. There are also a large number of additional sequences from free-living strains, which are scattered throughout the tree. Many of these in the basidiolichen clade are described as "mucilaginous overgrowth on rotting wood", which is similar to the growth habit and habitat of many basidiolichens, so these may actually represent lichenized strains, but the study that these sequences are from is unpublished so I don't know the details. This basidiolichen clade also includes unpublished sequences from photobionts of three <em>Solorina</em> specimens, which are Peltigeralean lichens.

Strains identified as <em>Pseudococcomyxa</em>, <em>Paradoxia</em> and <em>Choricystis</em> are nested within <em>Coccomyxa</em>, so it appears that all of these strains represent the same genus, with different species specialised on rotting wood (either in association with basidiolichen fungi or possibly free-living), Peltigeralean lichen fungi, and fresh water (often in association with <em>Paramecium</em>). However, this specificity is not absolute, at least for the two lichenized species. There are also one or more additional species that grow free-living and in association with with protists, but that do not appear to be able to lichenize, though additional sampling will be required to confirm this.

  <title>Green Algal Photobionts: Trentepohlia</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/07/23/green-algal-photobionts-trentepohlia/</link>
  <pubDate>Tue, 23 Jul 2013 10:47:07 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=301</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[While most work has focused on coccoid green algae and/or cyanobacteria, a largely overlooked lineage of lichen photobionts is the <a href="http://ucjeps.berkeley.edu/constancea/83/lopez_etal/trentepohliales.html">Trentepoliales</a>, a group of filamentous, carotenoid producing green algae. Trentepohlian algae are associated with one fifth of lichen species world wide and are particularly common as a photobiont of eipiphytic lichens in the tropics. Trenepohlian algae are also very commonly found as free-living colonies on tree bark, leaves and rocks. Like most of the groups discussed here, the taxonomy of the Trentepholiales has not held up to scrutiny with <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/19489121">molecular phylogenetic methods</a> and the various genus names do not appear to be meaningful.

The only published study that I am aware of on the diversity of Trenetpohlian photobionts is Nelsen et al. 2011 <a href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1529-8817.2011.00962.x/abstract">(J. Phycol. 47, 282–290)</a>. They found that lichenized strain are mixed with free-living ones throughout the phylogeny and that lichen fungi from different classes can associate with very similar photobionts. Since the publication of this study, a large number of additional sequences from Trentepohlian photobionts have been deposited in the databases. These includes sequences of both ITS, which is the marker that has been used the most widely in studies of Trebouxiophycean photobionts and <em>rbcL</em>, a chloroplast gene that is probably the most extensively used phyologenetic marker in plants. Since <em>rbcL</em> was used in the study mentioned above, that is what I used for the analysis presented here.

Methods are the same as those described previously. Because the <em>rbcL</em> is so highly conserved, long sequences from more distantly related algae had higher Evalues than shorter sequences from Trenetepohlian algae. This means that I had to manually add missed sequences with accession numbers that were bracketed by the ones that were found. It also meant that I had to exclude sequences from other algae that formed a large clade sister to the Trentepohliales in preliminary analyses. The detailed steps of this analysis are<a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130723/log"> here</a>. Datasets can be found <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/tree/master/130723">here</a>.

This is what the tree looks like:

[caption id="attachment_307" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/trentepohlia_rbcl_class1.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-307" alt="Trenetpohliales rbcL phylogeny color-coded by host class (green: Lecanoromycetes, orange: Arthoniomycetes, blue: Dothideomycetes, red: Eurotiomycetes, grey: free-living). Sequences recovered from multiple genera are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9. Clades correspond to those of Nelsen et al. 2011. Tree is rooted by Chloromonas sp. U80809" src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/07/trentepohlia_rbcl_class1.jpg" width="640" height="398" /></a> Trenetpohliales rbcL phylogeny color-coded by host class (green: Lecanoromycetes, orange: Arthoniomycetes, blue: Dothideomycetes, red: Eurotiomycetes, grey: free-living). Sequences recovered from multiple genera are in black. Black circles indicate aLRT support &gt;= 0.9. Clades correspond to those of Nelsen et al. 2011. Tree is rooted by Chloromonas sp. U80809[/caption]

This phylogeny includes the four major clades discussed by Nelsen, but there are also a large number of lichenized strains that branch near the base of clades 2 and 3. The new sequences also greatly expand clade 4 (from 3 to 14 photobionts) and add a lichenized strain to clade 3 (a <em>Strigula</em> photobiont, as predicted by Nelsen et al.). Non-lichenized strains are distributed throughout the tree, and there are two case where a free-living and a lichen photobiont have identical <em>rbcL</em> sequences. There are a few subclades that appear to be specific to a single lichen genus (<em>Roccella</em> photobionts in clade 1, <em>Coenogonium</em> photrobionts in clade 2), but the most remarkable thing about this tree is the phylogenetic breadth of lichens that share the same photobionts. Clades 2 and 4 both include photobionts of four different classes of Ascomycetes: Lecanoromycetes, <span style="line-height:1.5;">Arthoniomycetes, </span>Eurotiomycetes and <span style="line-height:1.5;">Dothideomycetes. Indeed, there are two cases of photobionts of <em>Pyrenula</em> (Eurotiomycetes) and <em>Graphis</em> (Lecanoromycetes) having identical <em>rbcL</em> sequences.</span>

This is clearly a group that is worthy of a lot more study. There appear to be a broad range of specificities, from species that can switch among two or more classes of fungi (in addition to living independently), to ones that are specialised on a single host genus. Additional sampling of the Eurotiomycetious lichens in particular would be helpful to determine the phylogentic breadth of hosts for many of these lineages. Extensive sampling within species would also be helpful to range of suitable photobionts for individual species.

  <title>Symbiotic Nostoc Revisited</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/08/19/symbiotic-nostoc-revisited/</link>
  <pubDate>Mon, 19 Aug 2013 16:54:34 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=320</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[In the three months or so that I've been working on this blog there has been some evolution in the methods I'm using. I though it would be worthwhile to revisit the first group I looked at to see if these changes in the methods affect my results. There have also been some additional sequences released since I started...

<!--more--> These methods are summarised here:
<ul>
	<li><span style="line-height:16px;">Blast reference sequence against updated nt database (E-value cutoff 1e-180, max target sequences 3000): 310 results.</span></li>
	<li>Extract hit accession numbers from results and use <a href="http://nebc.nerc.ac.uk/bioinformatics/docs/blastdbcmd.html">blastdbcmd</a> to acquire sequences from nt database.</li>
	<li>Use a <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/GetRedundant.pl">bioperl script</a> to duplicate redundant sequences: 521 sequences</li>
	<li>Use <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/GetGB.pl">Eutils</a> to download Genbank formatted sequences.</li>
	<li><a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/ParseHost.pl">Parse</a> Genbank files to extract information on taxonomy, host and publication source for each sequence.</li>
	<li>Check that all sequences from included publications were extracted and add any missing sequences: 48 additional sequences (this was done manually)</li>
	<li>For studies that submitted a single representative sequence for each unique haplotype found, <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/AddDuplicates.pl">add duplicate entries</a> for each specimen with that haplotype: 731 sequences total.</li>
	<li>Use <a href="http://www.drive5.com/usearch/">uclust</a> to group identical sequences and <a href="http://mafft.cbrc.jp/alignment/software/">MAFFT</a> to align non-redundant sequences.</li>
	<li>Use a <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/GetExcluded.pl">bioperl script</a> to identify alignment positions corresponding to gaps in a reference sequence, then use <a href="http://trimal.cgenomics.org/">trimal</a> to remove these from the alignment.</li>
	<li>Use <a href="http://www.atgc-montpellier.fr/phyml/">phyml</a> to infer a phylogeny and a perl script to <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/AddMetadata.pl">map</a> host information for each specimen onto it.</li>
	<li><a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/blob/master/Scripts/ColourTree.pl">Colour code</a> tree by host family and view with FigTree.</li>
</ul>
<span style="line-height:1.5;">Details of the commands for each of these steps are <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130819/log.txt">here</a>. Files created at each step are <a href="https://github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/tree/master/130819">here</a>. The results of this process look like this:</span>

[caption id="attachment_322" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/08/nostoc_rbcx_host.jpg"><img class=" wp-image-322 " alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host family (purple: Stereocaulaceae, brown: Lobariaceae, red: Peltigerales, blue: Collemetaceae, orange: Nephromataceae, pink: Pannariaceae, green: plant hosts, cyan: free-living). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/08/nostoc_rbcx_host.jpg" width="640" height="482" /></a> Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, coloured by host family (purple: Stereocaulaceae, brown: Lobariaceae, red: Peltigeraceae, blue: Collemetaceae, orange: Nephromataceae, pink: Pannariaceae, green: plant hosts, cyan: free-living). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

The total number of sequences included in the tree has increased from 547 to 731. These new sequences include photobionts of <em>Stereocaulon exutum, S. fronduliferum, Protopannaria pezizoides, Fuscopannaria leucophaea, Leptogium azureum</em> and <em>L. cyanescens</em> that form long branches at the base of the tree. The latter three branch with the early branching<em> Peltigera didactlya</em> sequences included in the previous analysis while the <em>Stereocaulon </em>and<em> Protopannaria</em> photobiont sequences form the sister group to all other photobiont sequences.

The topology of the tree is similar to the one produced previously, with a most symbionts of plants and lichens in the Peltigeraceae and Collemetaceae forming a large clade with Lobariaceae,  Nephromataceae and Pannariaceae photobionts forming a paraphyletic grade at the base. Like the previous tree, there is also a clade of specialist photobionts that forms a sister group to this lineage. The major difference is that a group of rapidly evolving symbionts of <em>Peltigera neopolydactyla, Massalongia carnosa, Stereocaulon tomentosum</em> and the flowering plant <em>Gunnera magellanica</em> that were nested within the main  Peltigeraceae/Collemetaceae/plant symbiont clade in the previous analysis now branch at the base of the main photobiont clade.

Many of the new sequences that have been added in this analysis are from<a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23184157"> this paper </a>which compared the photobiont diversity of closely related sexual and asexual species. They found nine different photobiont haplotypes in the sexual species (<em>Degelia plumbea</em>) versus only two for the asexual species (<em>Degelia atlantica</em>). This can clearly be seen by zooming in on the basal Lobariaceae/Nephromataceae/Pannariaceae grade in the tree and highlighting <em>Degelia </em>photobionts:

[caption id="attachment_329" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/08/screen-shot-2013-08-19-at-16-58-55.png"><img class=" wp-image-329 " alt="Nostoc rbcX phylogeny, zoomed in on " src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/08/screen-shot-2013-08-19-at-16-58-55.png" width="640" height="674" /></a> Lobariaceae, Nephromataceae and Pannariaceae photobionts, with Degelia photobionts highlighted[/caption]

<em>D. atlantica</em> is restricted to one photobiont genotype shared with <em>D. plumbea, Lobaria scrobiculata </em>and<em> Pseudocyphellaria crocata</em> photobionts and one shared with photobionts of <em>D. plumbea, Lobaria amplissima </em>and<em> Sticta fuliginosa, </em>while <em>D. plumbea</em> photobionts are much more diverse. This is a dramatic difference and confirms a hypothesis about the interaction of asexuality, photobiont specificity and narrow habitat requirements presented <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/20493269">previously</a>. However, the <em>D. plumbea</em> photobionts form two clusters of closely related genotypes, with one<em> D. atlantica </em>photobiont genotype in each cluster. This suggests that both species may have similar photobiont specificity determinants at a genetic level, but that codispersal has resulted in limited opportunities for switching to other closely related photobiont genotypes. I am sceptical that the ecological niche of <em>D. atlantica</em> is limited by the genetic diversity of its photobionts since photobionts from the two different clusters are much more likely to have different growth requirements than photobionts within clusters, but this is something that will ultimately have to be tested experimentally.

<item>
  <title>Another perspective on diversity of symbiotic cyanobacteria: 16S</title>
  <link>http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/09/24/symbiotic-cyanobacteria-16s/</link>
  <pubDate>Tue, 24 Sep 2013 12:14:01 +0000</pubDate>
  <dc:creator>heathobrien</dc:creator>
  <guid isPermaLink="false">http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/?p=340</guid>
  <description/>
  <content:encoded><![CDATA[Up to this point, I have been focusing on the <em>rbcX</em> locus for all investigations of cyanobacterial photobionts because it is probably the most extensively sampled locus and it is more variable than 16S rDNA. However, it is limited because some groups of symbiotic cyanobacteria do not have <em>rbcX</em> sequences in the database. These include  symbionts of the water fern <em>Azolla</em> which has traditionally been called <em>Anabaena azolae </em>and the photobionts of a variety of primarily tropical lichens that have traditionally been classified within the genus <em>Scytonema</em>. 16S sequences are available for both of these groups, as are sequences from a variety of other related genera of cyanobacteria. Furthermore, it is useful to compare the patterns revealed from analyses of <em>rbcX</em> to those based on an independent locus, often sampled from independent specimens.<!--more-->

Unfortunately, my usual approach of identifying sequences using blast isn't very practical for the 16S because there are (literally) millions of sequences in the database from metagenomic surveys. Since these environmental samples can generally include both free-living colonies and lichen fragments, they aren't of much use for the questions that I'm interested in here. Instead, I made a list of relevant accession numbers culled from the literature. This includes both symbiotic and free-living strains, focused primarily on <em>Nostoc</em>, but including a representative sampling of other genera of heterocystous cyanobacteria, as well as some outgroup taxa. This list is far from exhaustive, and I intend to add additional sequences to it as I come across them in the literature. Currently, I'm including sequences from the <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130921/included_studies.txt">these</a> studies.

Once the list of accession numbers was assembled (556 in total), the analyses proceeded as described <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/08/19/symbiotic-nostoc-revisited">previously</a>. See the details <a href="https://raw.github.com/hobrien/PhotobiontDiversity/master/130921/log.txt">here</a>. The resulting phylogeny looks like this (see high res. pdf version <a href="http://figshare.com/articles/Cyanobacterial_16S_phylogeny/806243">here</a>):

[caption id="attachment_358" align="alignnone" width="640"]<a href="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/09/cyano16s_tree3.jpg"><img class="size-full wp-image-358" alt=" Cyanobacterial 16S phylogeny, coloured by host family (light purple: Stereocaulaceae, dark purple: Basidiolichens, dark brown: Geosiphon, light brown: Lobariaceae, red: Peltigeraceae, dark blue: Collemetaceae, light blue: Coccocarpiaceae, orange: Nephromataceae, pink: Pannariaceae, green: plant hosts, cyan: free-living). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group.Grey indicates outgroups. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9." src="http://photobiontdiversity.files.wordpress.com/2013/09/cyano16s_tree3.jpg" width="640" height="345" /></a> <br />Cyanobacterial 16S phylogeny, coloured by host family (light purple: Stereocaulaceae, dark purple: Basidiolichens, dark brown: Geosiphon, light brown: Lobariaceae, red: Peltigeraceae, dark blue: Collemetaceae, light blue: Coccocarpiaceae, orange: Nephromataceae, pink: Pannariaceae, green: plant hosts, cyan: free-living). Names in black indicate genotypes found in more than one group.Grey indicates outgroups. Circles on internal nodes indicate aLRT ≥0.9.[/caption]

Notes:

Members of the Stigonematales (heterocystous cyanobacteria with true branching) form a monophyletic group, but they are nested within the Nostocales (non-branching heterocystous cyanobacteria).

Most of the <em>Nostoc</em> strains form a large monophyletic lineage, but there are a few strains identified as <em>Nostoc</em> that cluster with <em>Anabaena</em>.

<em>Anabaena</em> is paraphyletic and includes strains identified as <em>Aphanizomenon</em>, <em>Trichormus</em>, <em>Dolichospermum</em>, and <em>Cyclindrospermopsis</em>.

<em>Nodularia</em> and <em>Scytonema</em> each forms a discrete monophyletic group, though <em>Anabaenopsis</em> and <em>Cyanospira</em> strains group within the <em>Nodularia</em> clade.

Lichen photobionts that have been described as members of <em>Scytonema</em> form an independent lineage, sister to <em>Nostoc</em> (along with <em>Gloeotrichia</em>). This novel exclusively lichenized lineage has been <a href="http://www.eurekalert.org/pub_releases/2009-08/ajob-atf082109.php">given</a> the name <i>Rhizonema</i>.

Symbionts of the water fern <em>Azolla</em> form a monophyletic lineage within one of the <em>Anabaena</em> clades, supporting the traditional classification and earlier <a href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/15549268">phylogentic analyses</a>.

All other symbiotic strains cluster within <em>Nostoc</em> except for one symbiot of the flowering plant <em>Gunnera</em> which forms a distinct lineage along with three free-living <em>Nostoc</em> strains within <em>Anabaena</em>. There are also two symbiotic strains at the base of the <em>Nostoc</em> clade that branch earlier than a strain identified as <em>Cylindrospermum</em>, meaning either that an early branching <em>Nostoc</em> strain has been misidentified or that these represent an additional symbiotic lineage. Similar early-branching strains are also present in the <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/08/19/symbiotic-nostoc-revisited/#more-320"><em>rbcX</em> phylogeny</a>.

Along with photobionts of the Basidiolichens <em>Dictyonema</em> and <em>Acantholichen</em> and the Peltigeralean lichen <em>Coccocarpia</em>, several <em>Stereocaulon</em> photobionts group within <em>Rhizaria</em>. I reported <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/08/19/symbiotic-nostoc-revisited/#more-320">previously</a> that <em>rbcX</em> sequences from <em>Stereocaulon</em>, as well as from <em>Protopannaria</em> formed a distinct lineage sister to all other <em>Nostoc</em> strains. I suspect that these sequences also represent members of <em>Rhizaria</em>.

In contrast to the <em>rbcX</em>, where photobionts of members of the Lobariaceae,  Nephromataceae and Pannariaceae (<a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/content/297/5580/357">group A</a>) formed a paraphyletic grade at the base of a clade composed of photobionts of members of the Peltigeraceae and the Collemataceae, as well as free-living strains and plant symbiont (<a href="http://www.sciencemag.org/content/297/5580/357">group B</a>), in this tree group B forms a paraphyletic grade basal to a well supported group A clade.

As with the <em>rbcX</em>, Collemataceae photobionts are distinct from Peltigeraceae photobionts, though the sampling of Collematacerae photobionts is much less, so the pattern is not as apparent.

There is a <em>Peltigera malaceae/Nephroma arcticum/Sticta hypochra/Leptogium lichenoides</em> clade (also including several photobionts of <em>N. expallidum</em>, another trimembered <em>Nephroma</em> species), though it is deeply nested within <em>Nostoc</em>, almost sister to group A (I'll call this group of <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/05/08/an-in-depth-look-at-the-diversity-of-symbiotic-nostoc/">specialist</a> photobionts group C).

Photobionts of the flowering plant <em>Gunnera magellanica</em> form two distinct lineages within <em>Nostoc</em>, but in this case, there are 13 sequences from other species of <em>Gunnera</em>, none of which group with <em>G. magellanica</em> symbionts. Instead they form 7 other groups, three of which include symbionts of cycads or bryophytes. Symbionts of <em>G. megellanica</em> still stand out on long branches, but not as dramatically as they do for <a href="http://photobiontdiversity.wordpress.com/2013/06/20/gunnera-symbionts-do-not-cluster-with-lichen-photobionts/"><em>rbcX</em></a> and symbionts of other <em>Gunnera</em> species do not appear to be on long branches.

Overall, these results support previous findings in the literature regarding the distinctness of symbiots of <em>Azolla</em> and of "scytonemoid" lichen photobionts. They are also broadly consistent with my results for <em>rbcX</em>, except that the rooting is different within the main <em>Nostoc</em> lineage. In <em>rbcX</em>, the root connects to the "specialist" group C lineage, resulting in a paraphyletic group A. In the 16S, the root connects within group B, causing it to be paraphyletic. In the future, I hope to do a combined analysis of both genes to try to resolve this discrepancy.

Heath OBrien (2013). Another perspective on diversity of symbiotic cyanobacteria: 16S <span style="font-style:italic;">PhotobiontDiversity.wordpress.com</span> : <a href="http://dx.doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.806242" rev="review">http://dx.doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.806242</a>]]></content:encoded>
